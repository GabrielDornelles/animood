In "Summer Ghost," the poignant intersection of life and death unfolds as three troubled teenagers—Tomoya, Aoi, and Ryou—navigate their emotional scars and existential questions. Each character embodies a unique struggle: Tomoya grapples with academic pressures that stifle his creativity, Aoi endures relentless bullying that shrouds her in isolation, and Ryou confronts the harsh reality of a fading athletic dream due to illness. Their shared ability to see the enigmatic Summer Ghost—a spirit entwined with themes of despair and longing—serves as a catalyst for their collective journey toward understanding mortality and the weight of unspoken grief.

The film’s atmosphere, crafted through Loundraw's delicate animation, evokes a dreamlike quality, with soft, muted colors that reflect the characters' internal turmoil. This visual storytelling complements the narrative's emotional depth, inviting viewers into a world where the supernatural intertwines with the mundane, resonating with those familiar with the melancholic tones of works like "A Silent Voice" and "Your Name." The pacing is contemplative, allowing moments of silence to amplify the weight of the characters' revelations, making it particularly resonant for viewers seeking solace in shared vulnerabilities.

"Summer Ghost" is a meditation on the fragility of youth and the haunting questions surrounding existence, appealing to those in search of narratives rich in emotional nuance and philosophical inquiry. It resonates deeply with anyone grappling with feelings of isolation, loss, or the quest for meaning, making it a must-watch for those longing for a reflective cinematic experience.
